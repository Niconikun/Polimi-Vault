The **Law of Conservation of [[Angular Momentum]]** states:

> If the net external [[Torque]] acting on a system is zero, the total [[Angular Momentum]] of the system remains constant in both magnitude and direction.

---

### 5. Mathematical Statement of Conservation

#### **5.1 For a Single Particle**

The [[torque]] about point $O$ is defined as $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}$. [[Newton's second law]] for [[Rotation]] [[Time]] is:

$$
\boldsymbol{\tau} = \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt}
$$

In component form using Einstein notation:
$$
\tau^i = \frac{dL^i}{dt}
$$

The conservation law follows directly: if the net [[torque]] is zero ($\tau^i = 0$), then:

$$
\boxed{\frac{dL^i}{dt} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad L^i = \text{constant}}
$$

#### **5.2 For a System of Particles**

For a system, we must consider both internal and external torques. The total [[Angular Momentum]] is conserved if the net *external* [[torque]] is zero.

$$
\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{ext}} = \frac{d\mathbf{L}_{\text{total}}}{dt}
$$

If $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{ext}} = 0$, then:

$$
\boxed{\frac{dL^i_{\text{total}}}{dt} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad L^i_{\text{total}} = \text{constant}}
$$

This holds even if there are [[Internal Forces]], provided they are central forces (acting along the line joining the particles), which produce zero net internal [[torque]].
