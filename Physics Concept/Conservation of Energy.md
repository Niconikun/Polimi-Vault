### **The Law of Conservation of Energy**

#### **1. Formal Statement**

The Law of Conservation of Energy is a fundamental principle of physics which states that:

**The total energy of an [[isolated system]] remains constant over time. It is said to be conserved over time. Energy can neither be created nor destroyed; rather, it can only be transformed from one form to another or transferred from one object to another.**

#### **2. Key Conceptual Components**

*   **[[Isolated System]]:** This is a critical precondition. An [[isolated system]] is one that does not interact with its surroundings; no energy in any form ([[Heat]], work, matter) is transferred across its boundary. For a non-isolated system, energy conservation applies to the system *and* its environment combined.

*   **Total Energy:** This is the sum of all forms of energy present within the system. These forms include, but are not limited to:
    *   **[[Kinetic Energy]] ($E_k$):** Energy due to motion.
    *   **[[Potential Energy]] ($E_p$):** Energy stored in a system due to the position or configuration of objects within a force field (e.g., gravitational, elastic, electrostatic).
    *   **[[Internal Energy]] ($U$):** The sum of microscopic kinetic and potential energies associated with the random motions and interactions of the atoms and molecules within a system. This includes thermal energy.
    *   **[[Chemical Energy]]:** Energy stored in the bonds of chemical compounds.
    *   **[[Nuclear Energy]]:** Energy stored in the nuclei of atoms.
    *   **[[Radiant Energy]]:** Energy carried by electromagnetic radiation.

*   **[[Transformation]] and Transfer:** The constancy of the total energy does not imply that the energy distribution within the system is static. Energy is continuously converted from one form to another (e.g., potential energy to [[Kinetic Energy]] in a falling object) and transferred between subsystems (e.g., via [[Work]] or [[Heat]]).

#### **3. Mathematical Formulation**

The mathematical expression of energy conservation depends on the context.

*   **For a Mechanical System (No Dissipation):**
    In an ideal, conservative system where non-conservative forces (like friction) are absent, the sum of kinetic and potential energy is constant.
    $$ E_{\text{total}} = E_k + E_p = \text{constant} $$
    This can also be written as:
    $$ \Delta E_k + \Delta E_p = 0 $$
    where $\Delta$ denotes a change.

*   **For a General System (The [[First Law of Thermodynamics]]):**
    This is the most universal and powerful expression of energy conservation. It states that the change in the [[internal energy]] ($\Delta U$) of a system is equal to the [[Heat]] ($Q$) added to the system *minus* the work ($W$) done *by* the system on its surroundings.
    $$ \Delta U = Q - W $$
    This formulation explicitly accounts for energy transfer via heat and work, linking the microscopic [[internal energy]] with macroscopic transfers.

*   **In the Framework of Lagrangian and [[Hamiltonian Mechanics]]:**
    The conservation of energy arises from a fundamental [[symmetry]] of nature: the **homogeneity of [[Time]]**. This means the laws of physics are identical at every moment in time. According to [[Noether's Theorem]], this temporal [[Symmetry]] implies the conservation of energy. In [[Hamiltonian Mechanics]], if the Hamiltonian ($H$), which often represents the total energy, is not an explicit function of time ($\partial H/\partial t = 0$), then it is a constant of motion.
    $$ \frac{dH}{dt} = 0 $$

#### **4. Scope and Validity**

The Law of Conservation of Energy is one of the most robust and widely applicable laws in all of science. It holds true in:
*   Classical Mechanics
*   Thermodynamics
*   [[Electromagnetism]]
*   Chemical Reactions
*   Nuclear Physics
*   General Relativity (with a suitably defined energy-momentum tensor)
*   Quantum Mechanics

In particle physics, energy conservation is linked with mass conservation through Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = mc^2$, allowing for the conversion of mass into energy and vice versa, as in nuclear reactions.

***

**In summary, the Law of Conservation of Energy is a universal principle dictating that for any process occurring within an [[isolated system]], the total energy before the process must equal the total energy after the process, despite any transformations between its various forms.**
