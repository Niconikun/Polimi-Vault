## Formal Definition

The **Mach Number** (M) is a dimensionless quantity representing the ratio of the flow velocity (v) of a fluid to the local **speed of sound** (a) in that same medium. It is not an absolute speed; because the speed of sound changes with temperature, a Mach 1 flight at sea level is significantly faster than a Mach 1 flight in the cold upper atmosphere. In spacecraft propulsion, the Mach number determines the pressure and temperature ratios throughout the nozzle.

## Historical Development

### Key Milestones

- **1887:** **Ernst Mach**, an Austrian physicist, publishes his research on the shock waves produced by a moving projectile, captured using spark photography.
    
- **1929:** **Jakob Ackeret**, a Swiss engineer, formally proposes the term "Mach Number" to honor Ernst Mach's pioneering work in supersonics.
    
- **1947:** **Chuck Yeager** pilots the Bell X-1 to become the first human to break the "Sound Barrier" (Mach 1) in level flight.
    
- **1960s:** The development of the **Space Shuttle** and **Apollo** capsules requires modeling Mach numbers as high as **Mach 25** during atmospheric re-entry.
    

### Foundational Contributors

1. **Ernst Mach:** The first to visualize and analyze the conical shock waves (Mach cones) created by supersonic objects.
    
2. **Ludwig Prandtl:** Developed the mathematical theories for lift and drag in subsonic and supersonic flows.

## Mathematical Formulation

### 1. The Core Equation

M=av​

### 2. The Local Speed of Sound (a)

For an ideal gas, the speed of sound depends primarily on the square root of the absolute temperature (T):

a=γRT

- γ: **[[Specific Heat Ratio]]** (≈1.4 for air).
    
- R: **Specific Gas Constant**.
    

### 3. Mach Regime Classification

Flows are categorized based on their Mach number:

- **Subsonic (M<1):** Information (pressure waves) travels faster than the object.
    
- **Transonic (0.8<M<1.2):** Both subsonic and supersonic flow coexist on the same body.
    
- **Sonic (M=1):** The object is traveling at exactly the speed of sound.
    
- **Supersonic (1<M<5):** The object travels faster than the pressure waves it creates.
    
- **Hypersonic (M>5):** High-energy flows where the air begins to chemically react (dissociate).

## Fundamental Principles

### Core Assumptions

1. **Continuum Flow:** Assumes the gas is dense enough to be treated as a continuous fluid (not applicable in the extreme [[vacuum]] of the upper atmosphere).
    
2. **Compressibility:** Below **Mach 0.3**, air is often treated as incompressible (M≈0). Above this, the change in density must be accounted for.
    

### Governing Laws

- **[[Isentropic Process]] Flow Relations:** These equations use the Mach number as the independent variable to calculate how pressure and temperature drop as gas accelerates.

## Theoretical Framework

### The Mach Cone

When M>1, the sound waves produced by the object cannot move "ahead" of it. Instead, they pile up into a conical wave front called a **Mach Cone**. The angle of this cone (μ) is directly related to the Mach number:

sin(μ)=M1​

- As M increases, the Mach cone becomes "sharper" and narrower.
    

## Physical Interpretation

### The "Screaming Runner" Analogy

Imagine a runner on a track.

- **Subsonic (M<0.5):** The runner is screaming. People ahead of him hear him coming and move out of the way. The "information" (sound) reaches the air before the runner does.
    
- **Supersonic (M>1):** The runner is faster than his own voice. He passes the people before they ever hear his scream. The air is "surprised" by his arrival, leading to a violent adjustment called a **Shock Wave**.

## Advantages and Limitations

### Strengths

1. **Similarity Parameter:** Two different objects (like a model in a wind tunnel and a real rocket) will behave exactly the same if their Mach numbers are identical.
    
2. **Nozzle Design:** The **[[De Laval Nozzle]]** works by forcing the flow to reach M=1 at the throat and then expanding it to M>1 in the bell.
    

### Limitations

1. **Temperature Sensitivity:** Because a∝T, a Mach number doesn't tell you the "true" speed unless you also know the temperature of the gas.
    
2. **High-Altitude Errors:** At the edge of space, where air is very thin, the concept of a "speed of sound" becomes less meaningful.
    

## Applications

### Spacecraft & Rockets

1. **Rocket Nozzles:** Designing the area ratio (Aexit​/Athroat​) to achieve the highest possible **Exit Mach Number**, which maximizes thrust.
    
2. **Max Q (Maximum Dynamic Pressure):** The point during launch where the combination of velocity (Mach number) and air density creates the highest structural stress on the rocket.
    
3. **Re-entry Vehicles:** Managing the intense heat generated by **Hypersonic Mach numbers** as a capsule returns to Earth.
