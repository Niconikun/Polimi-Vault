## Formal Definition

**White Noise** is an idealized stationary [[Stochastic Process]] characterized by a constant [[Spacecraft Attitude Dynamics/Power Spectral Density (PSD)]] across all frequencies. The term is an analogy to "white light," which contains all visible frequencies of light. Mathematically, it is a process with zero mean and a correlation function represented by a Dirac delta function, meaning that any two values of the process taken at different times are completely uncorrelated, no matter how close those times are.

## Historical Development

### Key Milestones

- **1920s:** Harry Nyquist and John B. Johnson at Bell Labs study thermal noise in resistors, providing the first physical observation of what would be called "Johnson–Nyquist noise."
    
- **1930s:** Norbert Wiener develops the mathematical theory of generalized [[harmonic analysis]], providing a rigorous framework for processes with infinite power.
    
- **1940s:** During WWII, white noise models become essential for radar detection and electronic countermeasure development.
    
- **1960s:** White noise becomes the standard "input" for simulating random launch vibrations in aerospace engineering.
    

### Foundational Contributors

1. **Harry Nyquist (1889–1976):** Established the relationship between temperature and the spectral density of thermal noise.
    
2. **John B. Johnson (1887–1970):** Experimentally discovered the noise generated by the thermal agitation of electrons.
    
3. **Norbert Wiener (1894–1964):** Provided the mathematical formalization via the [[Wiener process]] (where white noise is the generalized derivative).
    

## Mathematical Formulation

### General Form (Continuous Time)

For a white noise process $w(t)$ with spectral intensity $S_0$, the [[Autocorrelation Function (ACF)]] $R_{ww}(\tau)$ is:

$$R_{ww}(\tau) = S_0 \delta(\tau)$$

where $\delta(\tau)$ is the Dirac delta function.

### Frequency Domain (PSD)

By the [[Wiener-Khinchin Theorem]], the [[Spacecraft Attitude Dynamics/Power Spectral Density (PSD)]] is the [[Fourier Transform]] of the autocorrelation:

$$S_{ww}(f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} S_0 \delta(\tau) e^{-i 2\pi f \tau} \, d\tau = S_0$$

### Component Breakdown

- **$S_0$:** The constant power spectral density level (Units: $[Units^2/Hz]$).
    
- **$\delta(\tau)$:** The Dirac delta, which is zero everywhere except at $\tau=0$, where it is infinite.
    
- **Uncorrelated Nature:** $E[w(t_1)w(t_2)] = 0$ for all $t_1 \neq t_2$.
    

## Fundamental Principles

### Core Assumptions

1. **Infinite Bandwidth:** The process contains all frequencies from $0$ to $\infty$.
    
2. **Infinite Power:** Because the area under a flat PSD from $0$ to $\infty$ is infinite, pure white noise is physically impossible and exists only as a mathematical abstraction.
    
3. **[[Stationarity]]:** The statistical properties (mean and PSD) do not change over time.
    

### Governing Laws/Theorems

- **[[Central Limit Theorem]]:** Physical "colored" noise often tends toward white noise when it is the result of many independent random events.
    

## Theoretical Framework

### Band-Limited White Noise

Since infinite power is unphysical, engineers use **Band-Limited White Noise**, where the PSD is constant ($S_0$) up to a cutoff frequency $f_c$ and zero thereafter.

- **Total Power (Variance):** $\sigma^2 = S_0 \cdot f_c$.
    

### Relationship to Other Concepts

1. **Connection to [[Random Vibration]]:**
    
    - White noise is used as a "worst-case" excitation to identify all [[Resonance]] peaks of a structure simultaneously.
        
2. **Connection to the [[Wiener Process]]:**
    
    - White noise is the formal derivative of [[Brownian motion]]. While [[Brownian motion]] is continuous, white noise is highly discontinuous (nowhere differentiable).
        

## Physical Interpretation

### Significance in Space Structures

- **Launch Simulation:** Rocket acoustic loads are often modeled as broadband random noise. While not perfectly "white," they are flat enough across the structure's sensitive frequency range to be treated as such.
    
- **System Identification:** By exciting a satellite with white noise on a shaker table, engineers can measure the [[Frequency Response Function (FRF)]] (FRF) and identify every mode of the structure.
    

## Classification and Variants

1. **[[Gaussian White Noise]] (AWGN):** White noise where the instantaneous amplitudes follow a Gaussian (Normal) distribution.
    
2. **[[Pink Noise]] ($1/f$):** Noise where power density decreases with frequency; common in electronics and biological systems.
    
3. **[[Filtered White Noise]]:** White noise passed through an LTI system to create "colored noise" with specific spectral shapes.
    

## Implementation Considerations

### Numerical Methods

In computer simulations, white noise is generated as a sequence of independent, identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables.

- **Sampling Rate:** The "whiteness" of digital noise is limited by the Nyquist frequency ($f_s/2$).
    

### Computational Aspects

- **Simulation:** To simulate a structure's response to random wind or acoustics, engineers often "drive" their Finite Element Models with a white noise signal and then apply filters to match the real-world environment.
    

## Advantages and Limitations

### Strengths

- **Simplicity:** The constant PSD simplifies mathematical integrations in the frequency domain.
    
- **Exhaustive:** It excites all structural modes equally, ensuring no hidden resonances are missed.
    

### Weaknesses

- **Unphysical Power:** Requires a frequency cutoff to be used in real-world calculations.
    
- **Mathematical Sensitivity:** Requires careful handling in [[differential equations]] (Itō calculus).
