## Formal Definition

A **Sun Sensor** is a navigational instrument used by spacecraft to determine the direction of the Sun relative to the spacecraft's body frame. Because the Sun is the brightest object in the sky and a critical source of energy, these sensors are used for solar panel pointing, initial attitude acquisition after launch, and as a "Safe Mode" recovery tool. While generally less precise than star trackers, sun sensors are highly reliable, computationally simple, and can provide a wide [[Field of View]] to ensure the spacecraft can always find its primary power source.



## Historical Development

### Key Milestones
- **1950s:** Early satellites like Vanguard use simple photocells to detect solar illumination.
- **1960s:** Development of the "Adcole" style digital sun sensor, which uses a coded mask to provide discrete angular measurements.
- **1990s:** Introduction of **PSD** (Position Sensitive Detectors) and **APS** (Active Pixel Sensors) allows for smaller, analog-to-digital sun sensors with higher resolution.
- **Present:** Modern CubeSats utilize miniaturized "Sun Sensor on a Chip" technology, integrating the sensor and processing logic into a single semiconductor package.

### Foundational Contributors
1. **Johannes Kepler (1571–1630):** His work on planetary motion established the Sun as the central reference point for [[solar system]] navigation.
2. **Adcole Space (Company):** A pioneer in the aerospace industry that standardized the high-reliability digital sun sensors used on hundreds of NASA and military missions.

## Mathematical Formulation

### The Sun Vector ($\mathbf{S}$)
The goal of the sensor is to output a unit vector $\mathbf{S}_{body}$ representing the direction of the Sun. For a simple 2-axis analog sensor, the angles ($\alpha, \beta$) are derived from the current generated by four photodiodes ($I_1, I_2, I_3, I_4$):

$$\alpha = \arctan \left( K \frac{I_1 - I_2}{I_1 + I_2} \right)$$
$$\beta = \arctan \left( K \frac{I_3 - I_4}{I_3 + I_4} \right)$$

### Component Breakdown
- **$I_n$:** The current produced by each cell, proportional to the cosine of the angle of incidence.
- **$K$:** A calibration constant based on the sensor's geometry.
- **$\mathbf{S}_{body}$:** The final vector in the spacecraft frame:
  $$\mathbf{S}_{body} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\alpha \sin\beta \\ \sin\alpha \\ \cos\alpha \cos\beta \end{bmatrix}$$



## Fundamental Principles

### Core Assumptions
1. **Point Source:** The Sun is treated as a point source of light (despite having a $\approx 0.5^\circ$ angular diameter at 1 AU).
2. **Dominant Source:** Assumes the Sun is significantly brighter than any other object (like the Moon or Earth-shine), which is true for the visible spectrum.

### Governing Laws
- **Cosine Law of Illumination:** The intensity of light falling on a surface is proportional to the cosine of the angle between the light source and the surface normal ($P \propto \cos \theta$).
- **[[Photoelectric Effect]]:** The conversion of solar photons into measurable electric current.

## Theoretical Framework

### Sensor Types
1.  **Sun Presence Sensor:** A simple "Yes/No" detector that tells the computer if the Sun is in view. Used for coarse alignment.
2.  **Analog Sun Sensor:** Uses the ratio of currents to determine the angle. Simple but sensitive to "Albedo" (light reflected off Earth).
3.  **Digital Sun Sensor:** Uses a slit mask over a detector array. The position of the light "stripe" on the pixels provides a highly accurate, noise-resistant digital reading.

## Physical Interpretation

### The "Solar Compass"
If a star tracker is a high-powered telescope used for navigation, a sun sensor is a simple compass. It doesn't give you your full 3-axis orientation (it cannot detect "roll" around the Sun vector), but it provides a rock-solid reference point. If a spacecraft is tumbling, the sun sensor is the first thing it uses to "stop the bleed" and point its panels at the light.

## Advantages and Limitations

### Strengths
1.  **Reliability:** Extremely robust; they are often the only sensors left powered on during a critical "Safe Mode" event.
2.  **Low Complexity:** Requires very little processing power compared to star trackers.
3.  **Low Power/Mass:** Ideal for small satellites and CubeSats.

### Limitations
1.  **2-Axis Only:** A single sun sensor cannot detect rotation around the line of sight to the Sun.
2.  **Albedo Interference:** Light reflecting off the Earth's atmosphere can "confuse" analog sensors, leading to errors of several degrees.
3.  **Occultation:** Useless when the spacecraft is in the Earth's shadow (Eclipse).

## Applications

### Spacecraft Operations
1.  **Solar Array Pointing:** Keeping the panels perpendicular to the Sun for maximum power.
2.  **Safe Mode Recovery:** If the flight computer crashes, the hardware is often wired to automatically use sun sensors to point the panels at the Sun to prevent "battery death."
3.  **Initial Acquisition:** Finding the Sun immediately after separation from the launch vehicle.
