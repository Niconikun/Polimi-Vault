## Formal Definition

A **Magnetometer** is an instrument used to measure the magnitude and direction of the local magnetic field vector ($\mathbf{B}$). In the context of spacecraft [[attitude determination]], it serves as a vector sensor that provides the spacecraft's orientation relative to a planet's geomagnetic field. By comparing the measured magnetic field in the body frame to a mathematical reference model (such as the IGRF), the onboard computer can determine the spacecraft's attitude. Magnetometers are highly reliable, low-power, and essential for missions in Low Earth Orbit (LEO).



## Historical Development

### Key Milestones
- **1830s:** Carl Friedrich Gauss develops the first magnetometer to measure the Earth's absolute magnetic intensity.
- **1950s:** The **Fluxgate Magnetometer** is perfected for use in aerial submarine detection and later adapted for the first satellites (Sputnik 3 and Explorer 1).
- **1960s–70s:** Magnetometers are used to discover the "magnetic stripes" on the ocean floor and to map the magnetospheres of Jupiter and Saturn by the Voyager probes.
- **Present:** Highly miniaturized **Anisotropic Magnetoresistive (AMR)** sensors allow for 3-axis magnetic sensing on a single microchip for CubeSats.

### Foundational Contributors
1. **Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777–1855):** Formalized the mathematical representation of magnetic fields and developed the first measuring instruments.
2. **Victor Vacquier (1907–2009):** Developed the fluxgate magnetometer, which revolutionized both geophysics and space exploration.

## Mathematical Formulation

### The Measurement Vector ($\mathbf{B}$)
The magnetometer provides a 3-axis measurement of the magnetic field in the spacecraft body frame:
$$\mathbf{B}_{body} = [B_x, B_y, B_z]^T$$

### The Observation Model
To determine attitude, the measured vector is compared to the reference vector ($\mathbf{B}_{model}$) from a model like the **International Geomagnetic Reference Field (IGRF)**:
$$\mathbf{B}_{body} = \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B}_{model} + \mathbf{\epsilon}$$

### Component Breakdown
- **$\mathbf{A}$:** The [[Direction Cosine Matrix]] (DCM) representing the spacecraft's attitude.
- **$\mathbf{\epsilon}$:** Noise and bias terms, including "Hard Iron" (permanent magnets) and "Soft Iron" (induced fields) distortions from the spacecraft itself.



## Fundamental Principles

### Core Assumptions
1. **External Field Dominance:** Assumes the planet's magnetic field is much stronger than the magnetic field generated by the spacecraft’s own electronics.
2. **Model Accuracy:** Assumes the onboard geomagnetic model (IGRF) is accurate for the spacecraft's current GPS position and time.

### Governing Laws
- **Gauss’s Law for Magnetism:** $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$, stating that magnetic monopoles do not exist and field lines are continuous loops.
- **Faraday’s Law of Induction:** The principle used by fluxgate magnetometers to sense changes in magnetic flux through a core.

## Theoretical Framework

### Two-Axis Ambiguity
Like a sun sensor, a single magnetometer measurement only provides information about two of the three attitude [[degrees of freedom]]. It cannot detect rotation (roll) around the magnetic field line itself. To achieve a full 3-axis solution, magnetometers are usually paired with:
1.  **Sun Sensors:** The Sun vector and Magnetic vector together provide a unique orientation.
2.  **Extended Kalman Filters (EKF):** Using the change in the magnetic field as the satellite moves along its orbit to "resolve" the third axis over time.

## Physical Interpretation

### The "Electronic Compass"
A magnetometer is the space-grade version of the compass on your smartphone. While a hiker uses a compass to find North, a satellite uses a magnetometer to "feel" the Earth's magnetic cage. By knowing exactly how the field lines curve around the Earth, the satellite can calculate exactly how it is tilted relative to those lines.

## Advantages and Limitations

### Strengths
1.  **Low Resource Cost:** Extremely low power consumption and very low mass.
2.  **Continuous Availability:** Works in the Earth's shadow (unlike sun sensors) and is not blinded by bright light (unlike star trackers).
3.  **No Moving Parts:** High reliability for long-duration missions.

### Limitations
1.  **Magnetic Cleanliness:** The sensor must be placed on a long **boom** to keep it away from the "noise" of the spacecraft's own batteries, motors, and electronics.
2.  **Distance Decay:** The magnetic field strength drops off as $1/r^3$, making these sensors nearly useless at Geostationary (GEO) altitudes.
3.  **Local Anomalies:** Magnetic storms or large deposits of iron in the Earth's crust can cause temporary "glitches" in the reading.

## Applications

### Spacecraft Systems
1.  **CubeSats:** The primary (and often only) sensor for [[attitude determination]] in LEO.
2.  **Momentum Management:** Provides the "B-vector" data required for **Magnetic Torquers** to fire correctly.
3.  **Science Missions:** Mapping the magnetic fields of other planets (e.g., the MAVEN mission at Mars or Juno at Jupiter).
