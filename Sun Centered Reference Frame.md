### **Sun-Centered Inertial Frame (SCI)**

**Formal Definition:**
The **Sun-Centered Inertial Frame** (also known as the **Heliocentric Inertial Frame**) is an [[Inertial Frame of Reference]] with its origin at the center of the Sun. It is used to describe the motion of planets, asteroids, comets, and spacecraft within the solar system.

**Axis Definitions (Common Convention - [[International Celestial Reference Frame (ICRF)]] aligned):**
*   **Fundamental Plane:** The mean [[Ecliptic Plane]] of a specified [[Epoch]] (e.g., [[J2000]].0).
*   **Principal Direction (X-axis):** Points from the Sun towards the **[[Vernal Equinox]] ([[First Point of Aries]], γ)** at the standard [[Epoch]].
*   **Z-axis:** Perpendicular to the fundamental plane, pointing northward along the ecliptic pole.
*   **Y-axis:** Completes the right-handed triad, lying in the [[ecliptic plane]].

**Key Characteristics:**
*   It is considered an **inertial frame** for most solar system dynamics, as the Sun's [[acceleration]] towards the galactic center is negligible for short-term calculations.
*   It is the standard frame for calculating and expressing planetary [[Ephemeris]] and for interplanetary mission design (e.g., trajectories to Mars or Jupiter).
