# Atmosphere

## Formal Definition

The **atmosphere** is the layer of gases surrounding Earth (and other celestial bodies), held in place by gravity. It is a complex [[Fluid Mechanics|fluid system]] composed primarily of nitrogen (78%), oxygen (21%), and trace gases including argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapor. The atmosphere serves multiple critical functions: regulating temperature via greenhouse effect, protecting from [[Radiation|solar radiation]], supporting weather and climate systems, and enabling life. Understanding atmospheric dynamics, composition, and chemistry is essential to [[Aerospace]] engineering, climate science, and [[Environmental|environmental]] studies.

## Historical Development

### Key Milestones
- **1643:** Evangelista Torricelli invents barometer; discovers atmospheric pressure
- **1742:** Anders Celsius develops temperature scale; atmospheric temperature measurement
- **1802:** John Dalton proposes atomic theory; applies to gas mixtures
- **1850s:** Buys Ballot discovers geostrophic balance; wind-pressure relationship
- **1900s:** Meteorology becomes quantitative science (Richardson, Bjerknes)
- **1957–1958:** International Geophysical Year; satellite observations begin
- **1960s–Present:** Climate science revolution; greenhouse effect quantified; ozone depletion discovered
- **1980s–Present:** Numerical weather prediction; climate modeling; satellite remote sensing

### Foundational Contributors
1. **Evangelista Torricelli (1608–1647):** Barometer; atmospheric pressure measurement
2. **John Dalton (1766–1844):** Gas law; partial pressure concept
3. **Christoph Buys Ballot (1817–1890):** Wind-pressure relationships
4. **Vilhelm Bjerknes (1862–1951):** Quantitative meteorology; frontal theory
5. **Paul Ehrlich (1854–1915):** Early climate change warnings

## Atmospheric Composition

### Major Constituents

| Gas | Percentage | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Nitrogen (N₂) | 78.08% | Inert; nutrient source (fixation) |
| Oxygen (O₂) | 20.95% | Respiration; combustion |
| Argon (Ar) | 0.93% | Inert trace gas |
| CO₂ | 0.04% | Photosynthesis; greenhouse gas |
| Neon (Ne) | 0.0018% | Trace; inert |
| Helium (He) | 0.0005% | Trace; low density |

### Trace Gases (Variable Concentration)

**Water vapor (H₂O):**
- 0–4% (highly variable; largest at equator, lowest at poles)
- Strongest greenhouse gas (by magnitude)
- Condenses → clouds, precipitation

**Ozone (O₃):**
- ~0.000004% (troposphere); ~3% of stratosphere (ozone layer)
- Absorbs UV radiation; protects life below
- Created photochemically; destroyed by CFCs (ozone depletion)

**Methane (CH₄):**
- ~1.8 ppm (parts per million)
- Potent greenhouse gas (25× warming over 100 years vs. CO₂)
- Sources: Wetlands, agriculture, fossil fuels

**Nitrous oxide (N₂O):**
- ~0.33 ppm
- Greenhouse gas; involved in ozone chemistry

### Particulates and Aerosols

**Dust:** Mineral particles from deserts, volcanoes; affect radiation

**Sea salt:** Ocean spray; affects cloud formation

**Sulfates:** From volcanic SO₂; reflect sunlight (cooling effect)

**Black carbon (soot):** Absorbs solar radiation; warming effect

**Effect:** Scattering/absorption of radiation; cloud nucleation sites.

## Atmospheric Structure

### Layers by Temperature

**Troposphere** (0–12 km altitude):
- Temperature decreases with height (~6.5 K/km lapse rate)
- Where all weather occurs
- Contains 75% of atmospheric mass

**Stratosphere** (12–50 km):
- Temperature increases with height (ozone absorption)
- Ozone layer peaks ~25 km
- Nearly still (low vertical motion)

**Mesosphere** (50–85 km):
- Temperature decreases with height
- Coldest layer; noctilucent clouds form
- Meteor zone

**Thermosphere** (85–500+ km):
- Temperature increases with height
- Air extremely thin; molecular dissociation
- Auroras occur in this layer

**Exosphere (500+ km):**
- Transition to space
- Essentially vacuum; satellites orbit

### Pressure Structure

**Barometric formula:**
$$
P(z) = P_0 \exp\left(-\frac{Mgz}{RT}\right)
$$

Where:
- $P_0$: Sea level pressure (101,325 Pa = 1 atm)
- $M$: Molar mass of air (~0.029 kg/mol)
- $g$: Gravity (~9.81 m/s²)
- $z$: Altitude
- $R$: Gas constant
- $T$: Temperature

**Pressure decreases exponentially with altitude:** ~50% at 5 km, 1% at 32 km.

## Thermodynamics and Dynamics

### Ideal Gas Law

**Air treated as ideal gas:**
$$
PV = nRT \quad \text{or} \quad P = \rho \frac{R}{M} T
$$

Where $\rho$ is density; $R/M$ is specific gas constant for air.

### Adiabatic Processes

**Dry adiabatic lapse rate (parcels without condensation):**
$$
\Gamma_d \approx -9.8 \text{ K/km} (1 \text{ K per } 100 \text{ m})
$$

**Moist adiabatic lapse rate (with water condensation):**
$$
\Gamma_m \approx -6 \text{ K/km (variable with humidity)}$$

**Importance:** Determine atmospheric stability; convection potential.

### Coriolis Effect

**Wind deflection in rotating frame:**
$$
\mathbf{F}_{\text{Coriolis}} = -2\mathbf{\Omega} \times \mathbf{v}
$$

**At latitude $\phi$:** Parameter Coriolis: $f = 2\Omega \sin(\phi) \approx 10^{-4}$ s⁻¹

**Effect:**
- Equator ($\phi = 0$): No deflection
- Midlatitudes ($\phi = 45°$): Strong deflection
- Poles ($\phi = 90°$): Maximum deflection

**Consequence:** Cyclones rotate counterclockwise (Northern Hemisphere); trade winds deviate; ocean gyres form.

### Geostrophic Balance

**In rotating frame, when pressure gradient balances Coriolis:**
$$
\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla P = -\mathbf{f} \times \mathbf{v}_g
$$

**Geostrophic wind:**
$$
\mathbf{v}_g = \frac{1}{f} \hat{\mathbf{k}} \times \nabla P
$$

Wind flows parallel to isobars; stronger where pressure gradient steep.

## Weather and Climate Systems

### General Circulation

**Hadley cells** (tropics):
- Rising air at equator (convergence zone; heavy rain)
- Poleward motion upper atmosphere
- Sinking at ~30° (dry; deserts form)
- Return to equator via trade winds

**Ferrel cells** (midlatitudes):
- Complex eddy-driven circulation
- Westerlies (surface winds)
- Jet streams (upper-level winds)

**Polar cells:**
- Cold dense air sinks at poles
- Poleward motion in upper atmosphere

**Result:** Three-cell circulation redistributes heat poleward.

### Weather Systems

**Cyclones (low pressure):**
- Air converges inward; rises
- Cooling → condensation → rain
- Wind rotates counterclockwise (NH)

**Anticyclones (high pressure):**
- Air diverges outward; descends
- Adiabatic warming → clear skies
- Wind rotates clockwise (NH)

**Fronts:**
- Boundaries between warm/cold air masses
- Cold fronts: Cold air displaces warm (steeply sloping)
- Warm fronts: Warm air gradually replaces cold

### Climate Patterns

**El Niño/La Niña:**
- Tropical Pacific sea surface temperature variation
- Shifts global rainfall patterns; affects weather worldwide

**Monsoons:**
- Seasonal wind reversal
- Driven by differential heating; land-ocean temperature contrast

**North Atlantic Oscillation, Pacific Decadal Oscillation:** Multiyear climate variations.

## Greenhouse Effect and Climate

### Radiation Balance

**Solar radiation in:** ~1361 W/m² (solar constant) × (1 - albedo)

**Earth radiation out:** Infrared (thermal) radiation to space

**Greenhouse gases:** CO₂, CH₄, H₂O, N₂O; absorb outgoing IR; re-radiate downward → warming.

### Temperature Regulation

**Without atmosphere:** Earth avg temperature ~-18°C (freezing)

**With atmosphere:** Earth avg temperature ~15°C (+33°C warming from natural greenhouse effect)

**Human enhancement:** Extra CO₂ (280 ppm → 420 ppm in 200 years) → additional warming ~1.1°C observed (2020).

**Projections:** 1.5–4.5°C warming by 2100 (depending on emissions scenario).

### Climate Tipping Points

**Potential thresholds with irreversible consequences:**
- Amazon rainforest dieback
- Permafrost thaw (methane release feedback)
- Ice sheet collapse (Greenland, Antarctica)
- Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) shutdown

## Atmospheric Measurements

### In Situ Instruments

**Radiosondes:**
- Weather balloon package
- Measure P, T, humidity, wind with altitude
- Launch twice daily from ~1000 stations worldwide

**Surface weather stations:**
- Temperature, pressure, humidity, wind speed/direction
- Extensive network (automated)

**Aircraft sensors:**
- Measure conditions during flight
- Valuable observations during ascent/descent

### Remote Sensing

**Weather satellites:**
- Geostationary (GOES, Meteosat): Fixed position; rapid imaging
- Polar-orbiting (NOAA, Sentinel): Global coverage; high resolution

**Instruments:**
- Radiometers: Measure radiance at various wavelengths
- Radar: Cloud/precipitation detection and structure
- Lidar: Aerosol and cloud layer altitude

**Retrievals:** Invert satellite measurements to estimate T, humidity, cloud properties.

### Atmospheric Chemistry

**Ground-based measurements:**
- Trace gas concentrations (infrared spectroscopy)
- Aerosol optical depth (sun photometers)

**Aircraft campaigns:**
- In-situ sampling; vertical profiles

**Satellite chemistry (e.g., OMI, TROPOMI):**
- Column concentrations of NO₂, SO₂, O₃, etc.

## Aeronautical Relevance

### Aircraft Performance

**Density altitude:**
$$
h_d = h + \frac{T - T_{\text{ISA}}}{\Gamma} \cdot \frac{1000}{\text{ft/K}}
$$

High temperature/altitude → lower air density → reduced lift and thrust → longer takeoff/landing distances.

### [[Atmospheric Drag]]

**Exoatmospheric orbit decay:** Satellites experience drag in thermosphere; altitude gradually decreases.

**Decay rate:** Depends on area-to-mass ratio, atmospheric density (solar cycle variation).

### Weather Avoidance

**Thunderstorms:** Convective updrafts (can exceed aircraft climb capability); hail, lightning hazard.

**Turbulence:** Clear air turbulence (CAT) in jet streams; mountain wave turbulence.

**Icing:** Liquid water droplets at subzero T; ice accumulation on airframe.

## Climate Change Impacts

### Observed Changes (1900–2024)

- Global temperature: +1.1°C
- Arctic warming: +3°C (polar amplification)
- Sea level rise: +8.6 inches (accelerating)
- Snow/ice: Declining trends (Greenland, mountain glaciers, sea ice)

### Projected Changes (2100)

- Temperature: +1.5–4.5°C (depending on emissions)
- Precipitation: Changes in distribution; wet regions wetter, dry drier
- Extreme events: More intense heat waves, heavy rainfall, possibly stronger hurricanes
- Sea level: +0.3–1.0 m rise (thermal expansion + ice melt)
