## Formal Definition

**Absolute Zero** is the lowest theoretically possible temperature, defined as **0 Kelvin (K)**, which corresponds to **−273.15°C** (Celsius) or **−459.67°F** (Fahrenheit). At this temperature, the thermal motion of particles in a system reaches its minimum, and the system's internal energy and entropy (in ideal cases) are at their lowest possible values. Absolute zero is a fundamental concept in thermodynamics and is the reference point for the Kelvin and Rankine temperature scales.

---
## Historical Development

### Key Milestones
- **1702–1703:** Guillaume Amontons, a French physicist, first proposed the concept of an absolute zero temperature based on observations of gas pressure and temperature relationships.
- **1848:** Lord Kelvin (William Thomson) formally proposed the absolute temperature scale, now known as the [[Kelvin scale]], which sets absolute zero as 0 K.
- **1906–1912:** Walther Nernst formulated the **[[Third Law of Thermodynamics]]**, which states that the entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero as the temperature approaches absolute zero.
- **2018:** Scientists at the University of Bremen achieved a record-low temperature of **38 picokelvin (pK)**, just 38 billionths of a degree above absolute zero.

### Foundational Contributors
1. **Guillaume Amontons (1663–1705):** Proposed the idea of absolute zero through experiments with gases.
2. **Lord Kelvin (1824–1907):** Defined the absolute temperature scale and established the theoretical foundation for absolute zero.
3. **Walther Nernst (1864–1941):** Formulated the [[Third Law of Thermodynamics]], which directly addresses the behavior of systems at absolute zero.

## Mathematical Formulation

### General Form
Absolute zero is derived from the **ideal gas law**, which can be expressed as:

$$
PV = nRT
$$

where:
- **P** = Pressure
- **V** = Volume
- **n** = Number of moles of gas
- **R** = Universal gas constant
- **T** = Absolute temperature (in Kelvin)

By extrapolating the ideal gas law, it is observed that as the temperature approaches 0 K, the pressure or volume of an ideal gas would theoretically reach zero.

### Special Cases
1. **Ideal Gas at Absolute Zero:**
   - At 0 K, the volume or pressure of an ideal gas would be zero, though this is a theoretical limit and not physically achievable.
   - Real gases liquefy or solidify before reaching absolute zero.

2. **Quantum Mechanical Effects:**
   - At temperatures approaching absolute zero, quantum mechanical phenomena such as **superconductivity**, **superfluidity**, and **Bose-Einstein condensation** emerge.

## Fundamental Principles

### Core Assumptions
1. **Thermal Motion Cessation:** At absolute zero, the thermal motion of particles (atoms, molecules) in a system is minimized, though not entirely eliminated due to **zero-point energy** (a consequence of the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle).
2. **Entropy Minimization:** The entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero at absolute zero, as stated by the [[Third Law of Thermodynamics]].

### Governing Laws/Theorems
- **[[Third Law of Thermodynamics]]:** The entropy of a system approaches a constant minimum as the temperature approaches absolute zero. For a perfect crystal, this minimum entropy is zero.
- **Unattainability Principle:** Absolute zero cannot be reached in a finite number of steps or finite time, as cooling becomes increasingly inefficient as the system approaches 0 K.

## Theoretical Framework

### Thermodynamic Implications
- **Energy Distribution:** At absolute zero, particles occupy the lowest possible energy states, and the distribution of energy states is governed by quantum statistics (e.g., Fermi-Dirac for fermions, Bose-Einstein for bosons).
- **Phase Transitions:** Many substances undergo phase transitions (e.g., from gas to liquid to solid) as they are cooled toward absolute zero.

### Relationship to Other Concepts
1. **Connection to Entropy:**
   - Absolute zero is directly linked to the concept of entropy, as the [[Third Law of Thermodynamics]] defines the behavior of entropy at this temperature.
2. **Contrast with Classical Thermodynamics:**
   - Classical thermodynamics predicts that absolute zero is unattainable, while [[quantum mechanics]] introduces phenomena like zero-point energy that persist even at 0 K.

## Classification and Variants

### By Temperature Scales
1. **[[Kelvin Scale]]:** Absolute zero is defined as 0 K.
2. **Rankine Scale:** Absolute zero is defined as 0 °R.

### By Physical States
1. **Perfect Crystals:** Theoretical systems where entropy reaches zero at absolute zero.
2. **Real Systems:** Systems with residual entropy due to imperfections or multiple ground states.

## Applications

### Engineering Disciplines
1. **Cryogenics:** The study of materials and their behavior at extremely low temperatures, approaching absolute zero.
2. **Superconductivity:** Many materials exhibit superconductivity (zero electrical resistance) at temperatures near absolute zero.
3. **Quantum Computing:** Low temperatures are required to maintain the coherence of quantum bits (qubits).

### Scientific Fields
1. **Thermodynamics:** Absolute zero is a cornerstone of thermodynamic theory and the [[Kelvin scale]].
2. **[[Quantum Mechanics]]:** Phenomena like Bose-Einstein condensation and superfluidity are studied at temperatures near absolute zero.
