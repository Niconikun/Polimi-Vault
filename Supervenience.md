### Supervenience

#Supervenience #PhilosophyOfMind #Emergence #Metaphysics #Reductionism
**See Also:** [[Emergence]], [[Reductionism]], [[Downward Causation]], [[Complexity Theory]]

#### Core Definition

**Supervenience** is a philosophical concept describing a specific kind of dependency relationship between two sets of properties: **higher-level properties** (e.g., mental, biological, social) and **lower-level properties** (e.g., physical, micro-level). A set of properties *A* **supervenes** on a set of properties *B* if and only if no two things can differ in their *A*-properties without also differing in their *B*-properties.

In simpler terms: **There cannot be a change "up here" without a change "down there."** The higher-level properties are fixed, determined, or dependent on the lower-level properties.

> [!quote] Core Intuition
> "There is no difference in the mind without a difference in the brain."
> "There is no difference in the image on a screen without a difference in the pixels."

#### Historical Context & Key Thinkers

Supervenience was developed in the 20th century primarily as a tool in the philosophy of mind to articulate a middle ground between hard [[reductionism]] and substance dualism.

*   **G. E. Moore** and **R. M. Hare:** Early uses of the concept in ethics (moral properties supervening on natural properties).
*   **Donald Davidson:** A key figure who brought the term to prominence in the philosophy of mind in the 1970s, arguing that mental events supervene on physical events.
*   **Jaegwon Kim:** The philosopher who most rigorously formalized the concept in the 1980s and 90s, exploring its implications for mental causation and [[Emergence]].

#### Core Principles and Concepts

1.  **The Dependency Thesis**
    *   **Description:** This is the core of supervenience. The supervenient level (the "upper" level) is **ontologically dependent** on the base level (the "lower" level). The base level has ontological priority. However, this does not necessarily imply that the upper level is *reducible* to the lower level.
    *   **Link:** [[Ontology]], [[Dependence]].

2.  **Modes of Supervenience**
    The relationship can be characterized by its strength and scope:
    *   **Weak Supervenience:** A-properties supervene on B-properties *within an individual*. An individual's mental state cannot change without a physical change *in that individual*, but the same physical state in a different individual could potentially yield a different mental state.
    *   **Strong Supervenience:** A-properties supervene on B-properties *across all individuals*. If any individual has a certain A-property, then any other individual in the same B-state must have the same A-property. This implies a universal, law-like connection between the base and supervenient properties.
    *   **Global Supervenience:** The broadest form. The global A-properties of a whole world (e.g., all the mental facts in the universe) supervene on the global B-properties of that world (e.g., all the physical facts). Two physically identical worlds must be mentally identical.
    *   **Link:** [[Modality]], [[Possible Worlds]].

3.  **The Supervenience Argument (Jaegwon Kim)**
    *   **Description:** Kim formulated a powerful argument that poses a problem for [[Strong Emergence]] and non-reductive physicalism. The argument, in essence, states:
        1.  Mental properties supervene on physical properties. (Premise)
        2.  If a mental property M is to cause another mental property M*, it must do so by causing its physical base P*.
        3.  But if M causes P*, and P* is sufficient for M*, then M is at best a redundant cause ("overdetermination").
        4.  Therefore, genuinely causal mental properties must be *identical* to their physical base properties, leading to **[[reductionism]]**.
    *   **Link:** This argument is a direct challenge to [[Downward Causation]] and is central to the debate between [[Reductionism]] and [[Emergence]].

#### The Intimate Link with [[Emergence]] and Complexity

Supervenience provides the metaphysical "scaffolding" for discussing [[Emergence]] in systems.

*   **Supervenience as a Necessary Condition for [[Emergence]]:** For a property to be emergent, it must first supervene on a lower-level base. You cannot have [[Emergence]] without supervenience. The emergent pattern is entirely dependent on the underlying interactions.
*   **Distinguishing Emergence from Mere Supervenience:** This is the critical distinction.
    *   **Supervenience** describes the *dependency relation*: "No A-difference without a B-difference."
    *   **Emergence** describes the *novelty and irreducibility* of the A-properties.
    *   All emergent properties supervene, but not all supervenient properties are emergent. A JPEG image supervenes on its pixel array, but the image is not typically considered "emergent" in the strong sense—it is reducible and predictable. Consciousness, however, is argued to be both supervenient *and* emergent.

In the context of [[Complex Adaptive Systems]], we can say that the system's global, emergent patterns (e.g., flocking behavior) **supervene** on the local rules and states of the individual agents. This frames the micro-macro link with philosophical precision.

#### Examples of Supervenience

*   **Mind-Brain:** The mental supervenes on the physical. Your belief that "snow is white," your feeling of pain, and your desires are all dependent on the states of your neurons.
*   **Biology:** The property of being "alive" supervenes on the complex biochemical processes of an organism.
*   **Social:** The economic value of a dollar bill supervenes on a complex set of social conventions, agreements, and printing patterns, not on the physical paper itself.
*   **Computing:** The software (the program's behavior) supervenes on the hardware (the states of the transistors).

#### In Practice / Key Takeaways

*   **A Tool for Clarifying Dependence:** When analyzing a system, asking "Does X supervene on Y?" forces clarity about what truly determines what. It's a powerful tool for cutting through confusion about levels of description.
*   **The Limits of Intervention:** If you want to change a supervenient property (e.g., a company's culture), you *must* intervene at the subvenient base level (e.g., changing communication patterns, incentive structures, or individual behaviors). You cannot change the culture directly.
*   **Not a Solution, but a Framework:** Supervenience does not solve the hard problems of consciousness or emergence; it merely provides a precise vocabulary for framing them. The real debate is about what *kind* of supervenience relationship exists and whether it allows for genuine [[Downward Causation]].
