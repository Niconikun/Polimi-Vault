### **Formal Definition**

> **Statement:** Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a [[Force]] that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

This force acts along the line joining the two particles.

### **Mathematical Formulation**

The law is expressed by the vector equation:

$$
\vec{F}_{12} = - G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \hat{r}_{12}
$$

Where:
*   $\vec{F}_{12}$ is the **force [[Vector]]** on mass $m_1$ due to mass $m_2$.
*   $G$ is the **Universal Gravitational Constant**, a fundamental physical constant determined experimentally ($G \approx 6.67430 \times 10^{-11} \ \text{N·m}^2\text{·kg}^{-2}$).
*   $m_1$ and $m_2$ are the masses of the two particles.
*   $r$ is the **distance** between the centers of the two masses.
*   $\hat{r}_{12}$ is the **unit vector** pointing from mass $m_1$ to mass $m_2$.
*   The **negative sign** indicates that the force is **attractive**, acting in the direction opposite to the unit vector (i.e., toward the other mass).

The magnitude of this gravitational force is given by the scalar equation:

$$
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

### **Detailed Explanation and Key Principles**

1.  **Universality:** The law applies to *all* objects with mass, from fundamental particles to galaxies, hence the term "universal."

2.  **Inverse-Square Law:** The strength of the force decreases with the square of the distance between the masses. If the distance doubles, the force becomes one-fourth as strong.

3.  **Principle of Superposition:** For a system of multiple masses, the net gravitational force on any single mass is the vector sum of the individual forces exerted on it by all other masses in the system.
    $$
    \vec{F}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i \neq j} \vec{F}_{ij}
    $$
    This principle allows the law to be applied to extended bodies (like planets and stars) by treating them as a collection of particles and integrating over their volume.

4.  **Equality of Action and Reaction:** The gravitational force is a mutual force. The force that mass $m_1$ exerts on $m_2$ is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the force that $m_2$ exerts on $m_1$, in accordance with Newton's Third Law: $\vec{F}_{12} = -\vec{F}_{21}$.

5.  **Application to Extended Bodies:** The law, as stated, is for point masses. For spherical bodies with symmetric mass distributions (like planets and stars), the law holds exactly if $r$ is taken as the distance between their centers. This shell theorem, also proven by Newton, is why we can treat celestial bodies as points located at their [[Center of Mass]].

### **Key Implications and Significance**

*   **Unification of Celestial and Terrestrial Mechanics:** It demonstrated that the same force causing an apple to fall also governs the motion of the Moon around the Earth and the planets around the Sun.
*   **Derivation of Kepler's Laws ([[Kepler's Time Law]], [[Kepler’s Second Law]], [[Kepler's First Law]]):** Newton showed that his law of gravitation necessarily leads to Kepler's three empirical laws of planetary motion, placing them on a firm theoretical foundation.
*   **Fundamental Force:** It is one of the four fundamental forces in nature and dominates the dynamics of large-scale structures in the universe (planets, stars, galaxies).

In summary, **Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation** is a quantitative, vector formulation of the attractive force between any two masses. It is a cornerstone of classical physics, providing the mechanistic foundation for celestial mechanics and accurately predicting the motion of bodies under gravity for all but the most extreme conditions, where [[General Relativity]] is required.
