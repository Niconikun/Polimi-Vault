## Formal Definition

The **Analemma** is the closed, figure-eight curve traced by the Sun's position in the sky when observed from a fixed location on Earth at the same [[mean solar time]] (clock time) every day over the course of a year. It represents a visual plot of the Sun's [[Declination]] against the [[Equation of Time]]. The vertical axis of the curve corresponds to the Sun's changing [[Declination]] ([[Geographic Latitude]]), while the horizontal axis represents the "[[Equation of Time]]," which is the difference between clock time and sundial time.


## Historical Development

### Key Milestones
- **2nd Century AD:** Ptolemy describes the components of the [[Equation of Time]], the mathematical basis for the analemma's horizontal shift.
- **1675:** John Flamsteed publishes the first accurate tables of the [[Equation of Time]], allowing the curve to be calculated precisely.
- **1830s:** With the standardization of "Mean Time" for railways, the analemma begins appearing on globes to help users convert sundial time to clock time.
- **1979:** Dennis di Cicco captures the first successful multi-exposure photograph of a full solar analemma over the course of a year.

### Foundational Contributors
1. **Johannes Kepler (1571–1630):** His laws of elliptical motion explained the "swing" or horizontal displacement of the curve.
2. **Jean-Paul Grandjean de Fouchy (1707–1788):** Often credited with first drawing the figure-eight "meridian" line on sundials to correct them to mean time.

## Mathematical Formulation

The shape of the analemma is determined by two primary variables $(\alpha, \delta)$:

### 1. Vertical Component ([[Declination]])
The vertical displacement is caused by the **Obliquity of the Ecliptic** ($\epsilon \approx 23.44^\circ$).
$$
\delta \approx \arcsin(\sin \epsilon \cdot \sin \lambda)
$$
where $\lambda$ is the ecliptic [[Geographic Longitude]] of the Sun.

### 2. Horizontal Component ([[Equation of Time]])
The horizontal displacement is the **[[Equation of Time]]** ($EoT$), measured in minutes or degrees.
$$
EoT = \text{Apparent Solar Time} - \text{[[[[Mean Solar Time]]]]}
$$
The horizontal width is caused by the combination of the Earth's orbital [[Eccentricity]] ($e \approx 0.0167$) and the axial tilt.

## Fundamental Principles

### Core Assumptions
1. **Fixed Observation Time:** The Sun must be observed at the exact same "[[Mean Solar Time]]" (e.g., 12:00 PM local clock time).
2. **Fixed Location:** The observer remains at the same [[Geographic Latitude]] and [[Geographic Longitude]].

### Governing Laws/Theorems
- **Kepler's Second Law:** Causes the variation in Earth's orbital speed, leading to the asymmetry in the loops of the figure-eight.
- **Spherical Trigonometry:** Used to project the Sun's path along the ecliptic onto the celestial equator.

## Theoretical Framework

### Anatomy of the Curve
- **The Large Loop:** Corresponds to the period when the Sun is in the Southern Hemisphere (Autumn/Winter in the Northern Hemisphere).
- **The Small Loop:** Corresponds to the period when the Sun is in the Northern Hemisphere (Spring/Summer).
- **The Crossover Point:** Occurs twice a year (mid-April and late August/early September) when the [[Declination]] is changing but the [[Equation of Time]] passes through zero.



## Physical Interpretation

### Geometric Meaning
The analemma is a physical manifestation of Earth's "imperfections" as a timekeeper. 
- If Earth had a **[[circular orbit]]** and **no tilt**, the analemma would be a single dot.
- If Earth had a **tilt** but a **[[circular orbit]]**, the analemma would be a symmetric figure-eight.
- If Earth had an **[[elliptical orbit]]** but **no tilt**, the analemma would be a straight horizontal line along the equator.

## Advantages and Limitations

### Strengths
1. **Navigational Utility:** Allows for the determination of solar [[Declination]] and the [[Equation of Time]] for any day of the year at a glance.
2. **Diagnostic Tool:** In celestial mechanics, the shape of a planet's analemma reveals its orbital characteristics (e.g., Martian analemmas are teardrop-shaped).

### Limitations
1. **[[Geographic Latitude]] Dependency:** While the *shape* of the curve is the same globally, its *orientation* relative to the horizon changes based on the observer's [[Geographic Latitude]].
2. **Long-term Shift:** Due to the [[precession]] of the equinoxes and perihelion, the shape of the analemma changes over thousands of years.

## Applications

### Engineering and Science
1. **Sundial Correction:** Many high-quality sundials include an analemma scale to allow the user to read clock time directly.
2. **Architectural Design:** Used by architects to calculate sun-shading and natural lighting throughout the year.
3. **Planetary Science:** Comparing the analemmas of Earth, Mars, and Jupiter to study axial tilt and [[Eccentricity]] influences.
