### **Formal Definition**
A **sidereal day** is the time period it takes for the Earth to complete one full rotation on its axis relative to the distant, fixed stars (or, more precisely, the [[vernal equinox]]). It is the true rotational period of the Earth in an [[Inertial Frame of Reference]].

### **Mathematical and Astronomical Context**

The sidereal day is approximately:

**23 hours, 56 minutes, and 4.0916 seconds** of [[mean solar time]].

This period is derived from the fact that in the time it takes the Earth to complete one rotation relative to the stars, it has also moved a short distance along its orbit around the Sun. This orbital motion creates the difference between the sidereal day and the solar day.

### **Detailed Explanation**

1.  **Reference Frame:** The key to understanding the sidereal day is its reference frame. It is measured relative to the **[[Celestial Sphere]]** and the **[[Vernal Line]] ([[First Point of Aries]], γ)**. When a specific distant star transits the observer's [[Meridian]] (reaches its highest point in the sky) and then transits again exactly one sidereal day later, the Earth has completed one 360-degree rotation.

2.  **Comparison to the Solar Day:** A **[[Solar Day]]** (approximately 24 hours) is the time between two successive transits of the Sun. Because the Earth orbits the Sun, it must rotate *more than* 360 degrees for the Sun to return to the same [[position]] in the sky.
    *   In one year (~365.25 days), the Earth makes one extra rotation *with respect to the Sun* than it does with respect to the stars.
    *   Therefore, the number of sidereal days in a year is one more than the number of solar days.
    *   This relationship is expressed as:
        $$
        1 \text{ Solar Day} = \left( \frac{366.25}{365.25} \right) \text{ Sidereal Days} \approx 1.002738 \text{ Sidereal Days}
        $$
        Conversely:
        $$
        1 \text{ Sidereal Day} = \left( \frac{365.25}{366.25} \right) \text{ Solar Days} \approx 0.997270 \text{ Solar Days}
        $$
        This calculates to the 23h 56m 4.09s value.

3.  **Significance in Astronomy:**
    *   **Celestial Coordinate Systems:** The sidereal day is the fundamental timescale for the **equatorial coordinate system**. The [[Right Ascension]] of a celestial object is essentially its "sidereal time" coordinate.
    *   **[[Sidereal Time]]:** [[Local Sidereal Time]] is defined as the [[Hour Angle]] of the [[Vernal Equinox]]. When the [[vernal equinox]] crosses the observer's meridian, the [[Local Sidereal Time]] is 0 hours. The sidereal day is thus the period between two successive upper transits of the [[vernal equinox]].
    *   **Telescope Operation:** Astronomical telescopes often use sidereal time to track celestial objects. Since the stars appear fixed on the [[Celestial Sphere]], a drive system that moves at the sidereal rate (15.041 arcseconds per second of solar time) will keep a star centered in the telescope's [[Field of View]].

In summary, a **sidereal day** is the true rotational period of the Earth, measured as the time interval between two successive culminations of a distant star or the [[vernal equinox]]. Its value of approximately 23h 56m 4s is shorter than the common 24-hour solar day due to the Earth's orbital motion around the Sun, and it serves as the critical time base for all sidereally-referenced systems in astronomy and orbital mechanics.
